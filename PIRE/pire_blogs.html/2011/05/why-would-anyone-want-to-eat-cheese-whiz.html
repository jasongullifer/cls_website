"Why Would Anyone Want to Eat Cheese Whiz?!"

By PATRICK JOSEPH CLARK on May 19, 2011 1:50 AM | 3 Comments | 1 TrackBack

It is now day three of my stay here in Beijing, and I must give my experience here so far a great big thumbs up.  Every meal thus far has been shared with kind, accommodating, and hardworking graduate students with impressive patience for my poor Chinese language skills.  Don't get me wrong, many of the students here are quite fluently bilingual.  They have at least enough skill to help me through my first Chinese restaurant, dining hall, or fMRI training session. Our challenges in communication don't seem to be a "language barrier" to me - more like a "language screen."  We still communicate fairly well - about basketball, music, or the universal perception that Leonardo DiCaprio's appeal to the ladies since Titanic has significantly decreased.  But there are some terms or concepts that simply will not transfer across our languages...

Yesterday, I was lucky enough to be treated to dinner and a delicious cake (Beijing internet isn't letting me post a picture) for my birthday!  At dinner, I was embarrassed twice over by The Birthday Song sung on both English and in Chinese.  Our discussion was casual, concerning everyday things and differences I've noticed between the U.S. and my new place of residence (I never realized Chinese people don't use soy sauce??).  Amidst our conversation, we realized that many foods couldn't be translated into the other's language.  For example, a tasty drink made from some sort of beans and water remains a complete mystery to me, just as the concept of Cheese Whiz drew many grossed-out looks from my new friends.

Despite my colleagues' English langauge skills, I am reminded often that I am immersed in a country where not everyone can understand my scattered explenations of concepts I've never had to relay before.  This morning, Ben and I went for a runto the outdoor fitness equipment on BNU's "playground" or athletic field/basketballcourt/tennis court complex on campus.  While there, we were spontaneously invited to work out with a decpetively strapping Chinese gentleman and his comrades.  Despite Ben's and my limited Chinese fluency, a combination of gestures and impressive demonstrations led to a lighthearted strength competition.  Ben and I acquired some new terms and hopefully some new friends!

As such, I will continue to keep my mind and ears open to new terms and concepts, and do my best to relate my experiences to my new acquaintances and workout partners.  Keep it up, Beijing!
