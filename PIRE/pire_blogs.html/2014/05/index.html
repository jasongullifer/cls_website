Three weeks down!

By NICHOLAS ADAM ANDEREGG on May 30, 2014 5:23 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks
So among other things that have happened in the past two weeks, I've learned that I'm really bad at updating blogs and I think that's just a fact of life that we're all going to have to accept! (Seriously, this New Post tab has been open in my browser for a week and a half!)

Anyway, onto things that actually happened!

The past week has been pretty productive and we've tested quite a few participants. Of course, like in any subject pool you have to draw from, there have been those that have fallen asleep or failed to show up at all, but in general, more people have been tested than not! In the previous week, I was not yet able to begin testing because of issues with the testing software, but now that that has been resolved, things are going pretty well. People are actually learning my experiment's pseudowords in the training portion of the task, which is great.

I've been attempting to pick up Mandarin while I'm here (because who would pass up an opportunity for immersion language learning), and I think if I can get past the first hurdle of learning the sounds of the language, I'll be able to make some progress. Hypothetically, spoken Chinese shouldn't be extremely difficult for an English speaker to learn as the grammar is much simpler, the words are generally much shorter, and in general, it requires no conjugations. However, the sound system is so wildly different that it's hard to even pick out syllables. Luckily, Chinese only has about 423 distinct syllables, so I figure I'll just memorize the phonetic representations of all of them and go from there (and a large portion are extremely similar to English). I feel like I'm making things more complicated than it has to be, but pointing at everything and looking confused is getting exhausting!

Last week, I got a tour of all of the psych facilities at this university and the coolest thing by far was their mock scanner. Unlike the mock scanner at Penn State, which really just simulates the bore that participants will be in, the mock scanner here looks exactly like the real thing, including the warning about metal objects on the door. 
I'm pretty sure I almost had a heart attack when I walked through the door, saw the mock scanner, and wondered why we were just allowed to walk in their with our cell phones, etc.

Overall, it's been a pretty productive two weeks. I'll try to update this thing more often from now on!

This weekend, I finally got to see Nijmegen. I have absolutely no sense of direction, so I never ventured farther than the lab and the grocery store without Megan holding my hand (metaphorically speaking). On Saturday, we went into the city center to visit the market. My jaw dropped when we rounded that last corner and saw the line of booths. I couldn't believe it was the same city I've been living in the past two months! The only word I can think of to describe downtown Nijmegen is charming. The architecture, outdoor cafes, river, and trees all combine to give the city a feeling like it was pulled right from a fairy tale.

Speaking of fairy tale, the highlight of the journey downtown had to be our brief tour of the castle that was built by Charlemagne. I had read about the building in a guide to the Netherlands my mom got me for Christmas, and I was so excited to tour my first castle! I figured I'd spend a couple of hours wandering down drafty, stone corridors and climbing the winding staircases of turrets. I failed to consider the implication of touring a castle that was built almost a thousand years ago. Megan and I spent about thirty seconds walking around the curved brick wall that was the only remnant of the Kasteel het Valkhof. I couldn't help but laugh at my naivety. 

All in all, this has been a week of accomplishments. I finally finished preparing my experiment, and I finally saw the city I've called home the past two weeks. If my next five weeks are as productive as this one, I'll know Nijmegen better than I know Philadelphia, and I'll have plenty of good data to analyze!

Grüße aus Mannheim!

By ABIGAIL PEARSON MASSARO on May 18, 2014 3:34 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

It's already been six days since I've arrived in Mannheim and I feel like time has flown. I spent my academic year last year in Germany and it feels so great to be back. Mannheim is a really terrific city but is much different from Freiburg--where I stayed during my year abroad--in that it feels somehow much more industrial. From nearly every point in the city you can see one of the big giant cranes that are spread out along the harbor, and while there are green areas in the city, I'm not constantly surrounded by forest and mountains like I was last year. (Freiburg, unlike Mannheim, lies directly in the Schwarzwald, or Black Forest.) Mannheim has its own quirks though: It offers a more modern take on German city life and is also home to many people of Turkish heritage, which I find really cool. There are places in the city that have streets of Turkish bakeries and restaurants and where all the signs are in Turkish. To me, it's reminiscent of areas like Chinatown in New York City and I'm eager to explore these areas more (and hopefully locate a really terrific Döner in the process).

What I really love about Mannheim is that it, like many other German cities, also has frequent farmers' markets. The one here is three times a week in the main square; everything from vegetables and fruits to fresh cheese, bread, and big flower bouquets is available for purchase. Molly and I went yesterday and I bought lots of fresh veggies. It was such a pleasure to be able to cook for myself with all my fresh ingredients and I ended up eating dinner with my Mitbewohner, or roommates, who funnily enough are two engineering students from Alabama. They don't speak almost any German (which, I'll admit is somewhat disappointing as I was hoping to practice my German at home with my roommates) but it was fun trying to teach them German phrases and spread some of my knowledge about Germany (for instance, buy anything you might need for the weekend ahead of time as almost everything closes early Saturday and completely on Sunday, and trying to find things like Lysol wipes or clothes hangers at grocery stores is an exercise in futility).

 

Another really tremendous thing about Mannheim is that it lies a mere 30 minutes by Straßenbahn away from Heidelberg. Molly and I make the trek out today and did a tour of the Heidelberg Castle, which overlooks the Neckar and the rest of the city. After going on a tour of the castle, we bummed around the city and did some shopping. 

I really enjoyed being in Heidelberg because it feels much more "German" to me. That is, it has the cobblestone streets and old façade look that I really want from my German cities; until I went to Heidelberg, I hadn't realized how much I missed that in Mannheim. Regardless of the lack of "German-ness" (as I would call it) of Mannheim, I'm still very much enjoying my time here and I'm eager to see more of what this city has to offer!

Arriving in Beijing

By NICHOLAS ADAM ANDEREGG on May 16, 2014 3:25 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks
Arriving in Beijing on Monday was quite a culture shock!

Surprisingly, I was not too jet lagged to start with. I think it helps that the time difference between State College and Beijing is exactly 12 hours, and since I didn't get much sleep before I drove to the airport at 4am Sunday (because I don't think it's physically possible to fall asleep before 11pm), any sleep I got on the plane from Dulles to Beijing was during nighttime in China. However, over the next few days, jet lag started kicking in, and around 3pm my body started to decide that even though I wasn't tired at all, I should desire nothing more than to go to bed.

Anyway, I am insanely grateful that the advisor here, Taomei, asked one of her students, Jie, to meet me at the airport, because without that, there's no way I would have found my way to the apartment that I am staying at. Unlike my experience with many European languages, where if you speak slow each person can probably pick up enough cognates and hand gestures to make some basic communication, it's impossible to break the language barrier if you don't speak Chinese here. Most of the population knows only very basic English, if any at all, and since I know only a few phrases in Mandarin, it can be hard to communicate my intent and all but impossible to ask questions. Overall, having people who can speak both English and Mandarin come along with me has been almost a necessity in certain situations.

The biggest thing I've noticed so far is that I certainly stand out as a foreigner. I've been told that the area I'm living in is made up of almost 100% Chinese people, so a white person is quite noticeable. Just walking down the street I've been getting quite a few intense stares of curiosity. I've gone to a few places for food a handful of times and even though I've only been here less than a week, the people working there already remember what I like to buy because I stand out so much! I've also had quite a few people timidly come up to me and ask if they can take pictures with me or of me and their kids. Since I'm fairly certain I'm not a celebrity here, I did some quick Googling and discovered that that's actually a pretty common experience among foreigners in China (apparently, the people who do this tend to also be tourists to the city and come from areas where foreigners rarely visit).

Most people I've encountered do seem to be  accepting of foreigners. There are some word that are incredibly hard to pronounce for an English speaker, such as baozi, which is a delicious little meat filled bread pocket. It's pronounced /bɑʊ.t͡sʐ/, and although I have no problem with the /t͡s/ (which is like an "sh" sound pronounced where you pronounce "s"), but going from that to a "z" sound is not something I'm capable of. Every time I struggle out my best pronunciation of the word, whoever I am ordering from is happy to repeat it slowly to let me listen.

The food so far has been fantastic. I've tried literally everything I can get my hands on (and that's a lot of food). Compared to the United States, food is extremely cheap. Baozi is my favorite food by far, and it can be had for about 2 RMB or 0.32 USD. Not quite as steep as my $2 muffin every morning in State College at Au Bon Pain. There's a nearby road lined with market stalls selling a delicious variety of foods; I'm slowly making my way through trying everything.

Meals in restaurants are also amazing, although they taste nothing like Chinese food in the US. They also seem to be very vegetable-heavy, which is quite a shock to a self-proclaimed carnivore like me.

Chopsticks are also the hardest thing in the world to use. Seriously. If America wants to curb its obesity epidemic, it only needs to replace all forks with chopsticks. Luckily, since I've had quite a few meals with Mandarin-English bilinguals, they've been nice enough to ask for a fork when I start to struggle!

I don't think this post really has a conclusion. And I think the whole thing was kind of rambled. But I'm still jet lagged and sleepy, and I'm totally going to use that as an excuse!

Arrived and Alive

By JENNIFER REBECCA KLINE on May 13, 2014 7:47 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks
As the plane descended below the cloud line, I saw my first glimpse of the Netherlands! Two words immediately came to mind: lush and wet. There are open pastures, leafy trees, and rich shrubbery stretching for miles. Scattered between fields and roadways are canals; at least, I think they're canals. These bodies of water that are too big to be considered streams but too small to be considered rivers crisscross the countryside. The landscape is beautiful and unlike anything I've ever seen in the US. 

Despite my absolutely awful sense of direction, I managed to make it on the train to Nijmegen without a hitch. I tried to keep my eyes open on the ride to the city so I could take in as much of the landscape as possible, but it was a lost cause. I did manage to note before I passed out with my head against the window how the line between rural and suburb seems pretty blurred over here. Fields of cows, sheep, and horses are located next to warehouses, towns, and train stations. From the window of my desk right now, it looks like the city of Nijmegen shares its location with a forest.

Speaking of my desk, the apartment Megan and I share is great! Megan met me at the train station, and the two of us took a ten minute taxi ride to an apartment building that looks like it's made of purple brick. All the doors to the apartment open with key fobs, so I feel really high tech every time I come home and wave my fob over the sensor to open the front door. We share a one bedroom apartment with a cozy living room and kitchen area. There's a nice, big desk where I'm currently writing this post. You can definitely tell this apartment is for visiting scholars though; the desk alone is almost as big as our kitchen! I think my favorite feature of the apartment is the funky bright red floor that matches our comfy living room chairs.

After I napped for a couple of hours, Megan showed me the building where I will be working. It seems really nice! There's a cool indoor garden outside the cafe where I'll get lunch sometimes. Megan then took me to buy some groceries. I've lived on campus the past two years, so this was my first time ever attempting to stock a fridge completely on my own before. Add in the fact that everything was written in Dutch, and it was a recipe for disaster! Luckily, I had Megan to help me out. She has been absolutely amazing in helping me get around here. I wouldn't be able to function without her. Kudos to all the PIRE kids who are going at this alone! I hope your survival instincts are stronger than mine.

While all things travel-related went smoothly, I've already suffered several technical difficulties. First, my personal laptop decided it doesn't want to work over here. It keep shutting down after a few minutes and now doesn't even recognize when it's being charged. Thank you, PIRE, for sending me abroad with two computers! The PIRE laptop works great; it's actually faster than my Mac. I just have to adjust from switching over from a Mac to a Dell. I had a minor issue with email yesterday too. For some reason, I was receiving emails without a problem, but I couldn't send any. Once again, Megan came to my rescue. Now I'm just hoping this post actually gets published. This is Take 2 of my entry, because the first one didn't save correctly. Now I know to copy a post in case it doesn't save once I attempt to send it out into the blogisphere. Live and learn! Speaking of learning, it's time to get back to my sentence splicing so I finish preparing my experiment. I'll be sure to post again soon!
