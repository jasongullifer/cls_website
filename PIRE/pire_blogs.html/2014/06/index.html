Finally days in Lund, Sweden

My last  week in Lund has finally come. It has been so easy to get lost out here and disconnect from world ( causing my lack of blogging ;) ) The people here live a calm lifestyle which at first I didn't understand. I was confused when I would get on public transportation and it was silent  or  see many people out to eat by themselves  ( and not pretending to be busy on their phones ) Back at Penn State I'm studying speech pathology. Every course that I've taken the teacher always reminders us that the first step is diagnosing a speech disorder is deciding if the patient has a "speech disorder or a speech difference." If your patient is pronouncing "dawg" instead of "dog" it might be because they have a problem with their speech or  they simply live in New York City and have formed an accent. As my time passed in Lund I began to apply this lesson to my life. Many Swedish people aren't going to make small talk on the bus with you not because they don't like you but because many Swedish people are shy and don't want to bother a complete stranger. Also eating  alone  doesn't make you anti-social or lack friends, some people simply want to some alone time that happens to be in public. I could go on and on about all the cultural difference I've experienced while aboard.  I think what I'll take away from this experience is that nothing is wrong with your own culture or the new culture you are in. It's just different and seeing these differences is a beautiful thing.

My roommates and I went to a traditional maypole dance where we tried to follow the locals in their dances. Later that night we had a big dinner at home. Once we were free from our food coma we went out to pick flowers for our flower crowns. The Swedish people believe that on Midsommars night you should place seven different flowers from seven different fields under your pillow. That night you will dream of the man you're meant to be with.  Unfortunately for me I dreamt of my mother! But besides that it was a lovely day and a successful Midsommar.

She is a great listener and never made a promise she couldn't keep. There was Anna, a PhD student from German. I've never seen a girl  eat so much and remain so thin. She spoke her mind and also took everyone's opinion into account. She made everyone feel welcome no matter where she went.  Lastly there was Kelly, a girl from the Atlanta. When I first arrived I thought we were have so much in common. As we got to talking we found out we had such different views and lifestyles. Almost every night we would eat dinner t

ogether. I think that's what I'll miss the most. Our talks over dinner would last hours discussing anything from the perfect time to have coffee to political views. The dinner table was just one giant melting pot of culture. No one was right and no one was wrong, just honest.

 



Friday I will finish my testing! I will end with 20 participants. Although it doesn't seem like a whole lot I am thrilled to end with this many people.  Battling against finals and specific  requirements made recruiting difficult at times.  I was informed that an abstract  I submitted to the Mental Lexicon in Queen lakes Canada was accepted ! Wir lieben Herzogenriedpark!

By ABIGAIL PEARSON MASSARO on June 23, 2014 12:15 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

I often feel that, after a certain amount of time in a city, one tends to forget what's great about that city. When I first came to Mannheim, everything about it was new enough that I was always excited to go exploring and there seemed to always be new things that I hadn't seen yet. Sometime around one or two weeks ago, I stopped feeling like there were new things that I hadn't seen and I was more content to stay in my apartment and do work rather than try to find new things to do or even different, local places to sit with my computer and get things done.

Yesterday Molly and I decided though that we would try to do a bit more sightseeing in our last few weeks and we went to Herzogenriedpark, the local park that lies halfway between our two apartments. I had wanted to go to the park before, as it was only about 15 minutes away from my apartment, but the entry fee always deterred me. Why, I thought, should they charge me to go into a green space? But once we got there, we saw it was in fact much better; Herzogenriedpark is an extensive set of grounds that has everything from a tremendous rose garden, to a sizable jungle gym with a climbing rope web that was nearly two stories tall. There was entry into a pool area with slides, as well as an area for minigolf, and even a petting zoo! (One of the goats was nice enough to smile for me when I took his picture.)

We ended up spending nearly four hours in the park, spending a large of that time eating blueberry cake and listening to Blasmusik (traditional German polka) with over a hundred senior citizens and playing in the big giant rope web. The weather was tremendous and the park has numerous lounge chairs that are available to park-goers to sit in and sunbathe by the fountains or gardens; after Molly left, I ended up grabbing a chair and sat out read my new copy of "Die Tribute von Panem" (the German version of "The Hunger Games").

I wish that I had realized earlier how great the park was and that it was absolutely worth the cost of entry for all of the great things that they have to do there--especially on a Sunday. In Germany, as with many other European countries, the vast majority of things are closed on Sunday and there's not much to do except stay at home or spend time outside. The streets are usually fairly empty and I always imagined that people stayed in with their families. I was wrong--it seemed like everyone in the city had gone to the park, just as we had. It was really enjoyable to see people out and being with their families in a green space and taking advantage of the great things that Mannheim has to offer. Molly and I have plans to go back a few more times before the summer's over--and I'm so excited!

Weekend in Milano

By ABIGAIL PEARSON MASSARO on June 23, 2014 11:56 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

One of the undisputed perks of being in Europe is that everything is close by (relatively speaking). My dad is currently living and working in Milan, Italy in a three-year position with his company. As I didn't have any participants on the Friday leading up to the weekend, I decided on a fairly spontaneous trip down to Milan to go see him! (Fortunately my father's company was generous enough to offer to pay for my train fare there and back, so it ended up being a super cheap trip!)

I always marvel at the stark contrast between geographic proximity and cultural variety that Europe offers. That is, that even though Italy lies just a few hours beyond the German border, the cultures are very different. As soon as we crossed over into Italy, the Italian police came on the train at every stop and walked up and down the aisles--something that has never happened to me on a train in any of the other countries I've been in in all my time in Europe. When I began to speak to one of the officers in German (we had just crossed over from Switzerland, so I figured that was my best bet), asking a question about my American ID, he stopped me mid-sentence saying, "I do not speak German. I do not speak English. You must ask someone else," and promptly walked away. While I of course do not believe that his curtness is representative of the larger Italian population, it was a stark reminder that crossing a border often means a change in customs.

Despite my run-in with the rather unfriendly policeman, I ended up having a really terrific time; two of my friends I met last year in Germany were also free for Pfingsten, or Pentecost, and decided to meet me for the weekend. We were able to fit a lot into the weekend--shopping and sightseeing in Milan and taking a daytrip to the nearby town of Stresa. Luckily the weather stayed nice for us (I even got a sunburn, despite predictions that it would thunderstorm the entire weekend). My dad, having already lived in Milan for several months before we came, was able to show us some of the things to see in the area and give us tips on what to do and where to eat.

My trip ended up also being a really nice break in the monotony of life in Mannheim and was a good opportunity to practice the ten words of italiano I know. (Those being the words for basic foods and drinks, and how to order said items--you know, the important stuff.) While I love practicing any of the languages I know (basically just German and un peu French) I often foret how debilitating it can be to not understand what people are saying. I take for granted understanding what people are asking me in Germany; several times in Italy when I was spoken to in rapid Italian I was forced to shrug my shoulders and offer only a helpless deer-in-the-headlights stare back, at which point they were forced to mime everything for me. I don't really find it embarrassing--there will always be languages I don't speak and it won't stop me from traveling and trying to experience the culture--but it sure is nice to be able to answer people in their native language and have my food or drink served exactly as I would like it.

Nijmegen, the Netherlands

By DANIEL JEFFREY MAGERMAN on June 14, 2014 11:07 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

First off, right out of the gate, as a budding linguist it seems appropriate to share that the city is pronounced NIGH-may-hen (and depending on what part of the country you're from there's some variation with the final "-gen"), but never a hard "j" in "Nij-." And that's about the entirety of my knowledge of Dutch. As many Dutch people have told me, the Dutch enjoy speaking English and can occasionally be impatient with foreigners attempting to practice Nederlands (or how the Dutch say "Dutch" in Dutch).  These interactions have happened of course, in English. Besides those minor and predictable linguistic differences, I have only good things to say about the people I've met so far -- in English or any other language. I've been in the country for about a week and half now, living (until tomorrow, actually) in a rented room of a wonderful host named Wibe (try pronouncing that one) near Nijmegen's city center. Beginning my stay off of Radboud University's campus has given me license to conduct a self-guided biking tour of the surroundings. What I've found are markets and parks and churches and trees and gardens and bridges with cityscapes and beaches and all of this appears almost impossibly beautiful. After we Americans accidentally bombed Nijmegen in World War II, mistaking it for a German stronghold (it IS very close to the border -- whoops), the Netherlands' oldest city had some cleaning up to do. And clean up they have. Old architecture and new, impressive infrastructure make Nijmegen a very nice place to live. 

view from the bridge into town

The bike ride to campus isn't too bad on the eyes either. So far at Radboud the majority of my work has been independently transcribing and coding the data I collected in Colombia. I have, however -- alongside my fellow field-researchers Mary Beth and Melinda -- attended some meetings with presentations by members from labs with names spelled in colorful acronyms: LinC, CLS, MPI, and BRC. Just yesterday, the three of us were invited to a PhD dissertation defense, here called by the more friendly title, a "promotion." The event was held in a cosy auditorium on Friday afternoon. The candidate walked to the front with her two advisors. Suddenly, a bell sounded and room silenced. We were instructed to stand as a procession of important-looking people paraded from the doors adorned in the garb of the tradition of Middle Ages academia. In other words, they were wearing wizard robes. (Here is an article with a more thorough description, plus pictures!)  The "examiners" asked the candidate questions about her work, mostly as a formality; she'd already made the grade. Although I didn't quite catch the portions presented in Dutch and Latin, after sitting through the ceremony and watching the new doctor of linguistics attach those three magic letters to her name, it was impossible not to get chills. 


There's more a'coming. So, keep your eyes peeled for the next post from the Netherlands.

Life is Gouda

By JENNIFER REBECCA KLINE on June 11, 2014 7:36 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks
Each week I'm here, I gain more respect for researchers. Talk about detail oriented! One small error can throw off an entire experiment. I've been running through my scripts over and over again trying to catch a missing letter or an incorrectly numbered sound file. Poor Sarah Fairchild, who's been helping me from State College, gets new scripts from me practically daily with an update of a new mistake I caught. I haven't quite figured out how I want to celebrate the day I make it through an entire experiment without an error message. My fingers are crossed that today is the day! It can be so disheartening getting an error message after spending hours combing through sound files to ensure everything will go smoothly. That's why it's awesome having Megan as a roommate. As a postdoc, Megan gives me a glimpse into the future I should expect as a researcher. Not only is Megan conducting her own PIRE experiment, but she's also working on a book chapter and reviewing articles. Last night, I watched her work on two computers simultaneously. If Megan can do all that, then I can overcome all the learning curves I'm experiencing with my single experiment. 

During the week I'm scouring Excel spreadsheets, but the weekends are typically filled with exploration. My friend Jackie has been studying abroad in Utrecht all of spring semester, and I spent one weekend with her. I've never had such a fun, spontaneous trip before! I finished my work on Friday early, and I decided to message Jackie to see what she was up to. Turns out, she was planning a bike trip from Utrecht to Gouda to Kinderdijk on Saturday, and she invited me to join. I slept over on Friday, and early Saturday, we went to the Dom tower and waited for her friend to join us. Her friend Zeynep had the common sense to tell us that attempting to bike to both towns was completely implausible. She convinced us to take the train to Gouda and then bike to Kinderdijk. Gouda was an adorable little town. Wheels of cheese are strung up between the buildings like Christmas lights, and cheese shops are everywhere. I was in heaven! There was no need for lunch after I sampled every type of cheese available in one of the shops. I was particularly fond of a type of Gouda cheese that was mixed with Rosemary. Before we made our way to Kinderdijk, I bought a small wheel for myself (I intended it to last a couple weeks, but I finished it in a couple days).

The bike ride to Kinderdijk was the greatest part of my trip thus far. We rode past fields of cows, sheep, and horses. Many houses kept goats in their front yards, and one home even had a yard full of alpacas! Still, the strangest sight had to be the goats we saw relaxing on top of a trampoline. The towns were all so quaint, and I love the look of the houses made of stone or brick. I've never been a fan of the colonial style homes that are favored in the US, and I could easily imagine myself living in any of the houses that we passed. When we arrived at Kinderdijk, the field of windmills blew me away (no pun intended). The old windmills lining the river could not have been more picturesque. I even bought a pair of slippers that looked like wooden shoes in the gift shop to round out this quintessentially Dutch experience. After about an hour spent admiring the simple beauty of the windmills, we biked back to Gouda to catch a train home. Google Maps said the trip would take an hour each way. Nope. The entire bike ride was four hours round trip. By the time I arrived back in Nijmegen, I was feeling every minute of that bike ride. Forget mattresses; Tempur-Pedic should be in the bike seat business. Everything from the waist down was sore. I'm woefully out of shape, and my thighs and calves hurt so much that I wasn't sure I'd be able to walk the next day. Luckily I was, but I definitely wasn't moving too quickly. The soreness was worth it though. I saw more of the Netherlands in that one bike ride that I have during my entire stay. I can't believe I get to spend two months in this absolutely charming country!

One Month Down, One to Go

By MOLLY ADA LICHTENWALNER on June 8, 2014 1:28 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

             Time is really flying by here.  It's sad to think that I have to leave this place in a month from today.  The past month that I have been here has been very enlightening and very successful.  As of right now, I have run almost 20 participants, have another 5 signed up, and am waiting for 6 emails back.  I have learned exactly how powerful Facebook and word of mouth can be!

            Other than running participants, Abby and I have gotten into a nice routine that keeps us up and out of our beds day after day.  Our day normally starts with stopping at a Grimminger café (they are on practically every corner) to grab my cup of coffee and head to our office and wait for participants.  We hang around in the office until both of us are finished running participants, and then we venture out into the city for groceries, or just to walk off that pastry we had at lunch time.  It's nice having someone here with a very similar work ethic to my own.  We are both very eager to get out and to explore, but we also understand that we are here for research and our participants and recruiting comes first.  Sharing the office can be difficult at times when we have participants scheduled at the same time (and you can hear through the walls), but we are both very lenient and always figure out ways to share our space.

            One thing that I find refreshing here is that everything is closed down on Sundays; even the public transportation runs a lot slower.  It's nice that people take a break once in a while.  It was a bit frustrating at first when I was in desperate need of groceries and there was no WalMart nearby to run to, but I have learned to really appreciate the peaceful Sunday atmosphere and have learned to stock up on food for the weekends.

            It's also been refreshing not having my cell phone attached to my hips at all time.  My generation is constantly on Facebook or texting friends, and not having that here has been quite a relief.  I've appreciated the silence and not the constant buzzing of my phone, and it's been a nice break from reality.  I've been able to experience things through my own eyes rather than my cell phone camera.

            I know that this next month is going to go by in a blink of an eye, but I am excited to see what the rest of this trip has in store for me and what else I can learn in that month.

Spain Week 3

By KYRA LEIGH KRASS on June 4, 2014 3:26 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

Hello all. Another great week has passed. Since I last wrote, I have tested 7 more participants. They all went fairly well. I have 2 more scheduled for this week which will bring my total up to 14 so far. I'm happy with that number, but I'm eager to get closer to my goal. I have had two people not show up in the past few days, so I'm hoping that does not happen too many more times. I only have a limited amount of time to schedule participants, so hopefully all my participants show up to their assigned times.

This past weekend was very eventful. I made it through the week and was rewarded by our trip to the beach. Nerja was beautiful, as I expected. Being that this is my first trip to Europe, it was my first time seeing the Mediterranean. I was very excited to see it from the bus window while we were driving along the coast. Paige and I stayed at a hostel in Nerja for Friday and Saturday night. We did a lot of wandering around the town, and we soon discovered that it is fairly small. It also has a lot of tourists, so we were able to hear many different languages being spoken by everyone there. In addition to spotting other foreigners in the town, we were also able to meet some new people in the hostel. Paige and I spent some time with people from England, Denmark, France, Australia, and Germany. The group of people consists of people of varying ages and backgrounds, but we were all tied together by our excitement about traveling throughout Spain this summer. Saturday was sunny and hot, so Paige and I got to enjoy the sun and the waves on the beach for a while. We also sampled some great foods, including waffles and ice cream and churros. Unfortunately, Sunday was a rainy day, so Paige and I did not get to spend very much time outdoors, but we had a very relaxing weekend as a whole.

This past Monday was the day Paige and I finally made it to the Alhambra. It was more beautiful, more intricate, and more breathtaking than I could have imagined. Up until this week I had only seen a few pictures, but they do not do the building and the gardens justice. The flowers and plants smelled so fresh and delicious, and the only thing that was more amazing than Generalife was being inside the Nasrid Palaces. The architecture was unlike anything I had seen before, and I could do nothing but gawk and take pictures. It is crazy for me to try to imagine that this building has been in Granada for so long. Paige and I were very lucky to have gotten to take a tour, and we could not have asked for more perfect weather either. I'm very glad that we bought tickets.

After testing some more participants this week, Paige and I are planning to head to Córdoba. We have been told by countless people that it is a beautiful place to visit, and we are going to take everyone's advice. We really are trying to take advantage of our weekends and travel as much as possible. I'll be writing all about Córdoba next week I'm sure.        

Adjusting to Mannheim

By ABIGAIL PEARSON MASSARO on June 3, 2014 1:51 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

Some of the initial excitement of being back in Germany has started to wear off and I feel like I've really adjusted to life here in Mannheim. I think that I was really very eager when I first arrived to see all of the things that the city had to offer. Molly and I have since slowed down and I think focused a lot on our studies and figuring out a routine. Everyday consists usually of a breakfast coffee (for Molly) and a baked good (for both of us). When we're not running participants, we typically stroll around or buy groceries all whilst discussing the merits of different sandwiches at local bakeries. I like that I've--we've--been able to sort of find a rhythm here; it makes Mannheim somehow feel more like a home, and not just a place I'm visiting.

I feel like I really lucked out too that Molly and I get along so well and that she, like me, wants to spend time in the city and in surrounding areas. While being with another person is not necessarily a prerequisite for having fun or appreciating a foreign country, I think that there's really something to be said about being able to share an experience like this with another person. It's advantageous, too, in that that other person can often be a motivating force behind getting up and leaving your apartment on less busy days. There have been several days in which I haven't had a participant scheduled where I may have been tempted to lay in bed or stay in my apartment with my roommates, but having another person around who serves as a reason to change out of my pajamas and spend some time in the city is something I think I really needed and appreciate having.

It's great too that she lives in a complete different area of the city from me. Because the commute takes about an hour (yikes) from her apartment to mine, I wouldn't have otherwise ventured out to that part of the city. I think it's easy when you travel to a new city to be somewhat hesitant to go to parts that are far away or unknown (perhaps if only for the reason that there's nothing terribly exciting or remarkable when you get there). I like having an excuse to see more of the city than I might otherwise. Maybe in the latter half of our trip we'll do a bit more exploring.
