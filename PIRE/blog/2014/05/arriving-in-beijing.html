Arriving in Beijing

By NICHOLAS ADAM ANDEREGG on May 16, 2014 3:25 AM | No Comments | No TrackBacks
Arriving in Beijing on Monday was quite a culture shock!

Surprisingly, I was not too jet lagged to start with. I think it helps that the time difference between State College and Beijing is exactly 12 hours, and since I didn't get much sleep before I drove to the airport at 4am Sunday (because I don't think it's physically possible to fall asleep before 11pm), any sleep I got on the plane from Dulles to Beijing was during nighttime in China. However, over the next few days, jet lag started kicking in, and around 3pm my body started to decide that even though I wasn't tired at all, I should desire nothing more than to go to bed.

Anyway, I am insanely grateful that the advisor here, Taomei, asked one of her students, Jie, to meet me at the airport, because without that, there's no way I would have found my way to the apartment that I am staying at. Unlike my experience with many European languages, where if you speak slow each person can probably pick up enough cognates and hand gestures to make some basic communication, it's impossible to break the language barrier if you don't speak Chinese here. Most of the population knows only very basic English, if any at all, and since I know only a few phrases in Mandarin, it can be hard to communicate my intent and all but impossible to ask questions. Overall, having people who can speak both English and Mandarin come along with me has been almost a necessity in certain situations.

The biggest thing I've noticed so far is that I certainly stand out as a foreigner. I've been told that the area I'm living in is made up of almost 100% Chinese people, so a white person is quite noticeable. Just walking down the street I've been getting quite a few intense stares of curiosity. I've gone to a few places for food a handful of times and even though I've only been here less than a week, the people working there already remember what I like to buy because I stand out so much! I've also had quite a few people timidly come up to me and ask if they can take pictures with me or of me and their kids. Since I'm fairly certain I'm not a celebrity here, I did some quick Googling and discovered that that's actually a pretty common experience among foreigners in China (apparently, the people who do this tend to also be tourists to the city and come from areas where foreigners rarely visit).

Most people I've encountered do seem to be  accepting of foreigners. There are some word that are incredibly hard to pronounce for an English speaker, such as baozi, which is a delicious little meat filled bread pocket. It's pronounced /bɑʊ.t͡sʐ/, and although I have no problem with the /t͡s/ (which is like an "sh" sound pronounced where you pronounce "s"), but going from that to a "z" sound is not something I'm capable of. Every time I struggle out my best pronunciation of the word, whoever I am ordering from is happy to repeat it slowly to let me listen.

The food so far has been fantastic. I've tried literally everything I can get my hands on (and that's a lot of food). Compared to the United States, food is extremely cheap. Baozi is my favorite food by far, and it can be had for about 2 RMB or 0.32 USD. Not quite as steep as my $2 muffin every morning in State College at Au Bon Pain. There's a nearby road lined with market stalls selling a delicious variety of foods; I'm slowly making my way through trying everything.

Meals in restaurants are also amazing, although they taste nothing like Chinese food in the US. They also seem to be very vegetable-heavy, which is quite a shock to a self-proclaimed carnivore like me.

Chopsticks are also the hardest thing in the world to use. Seriously. If America wants to curb its obesity epidemic, it only needs to replace all forks with chopsticks. Luckily, since I've had quite a few meals with Mandarin-English bilinguals, they've been nice enough to ask for a fork when I start to struggle!

I don't think this post really has a conclusion. And I think the whole thing was kind of rambled. But I'm still jet lagged and sleepy, and I'm totally going to use that as an excuse!
