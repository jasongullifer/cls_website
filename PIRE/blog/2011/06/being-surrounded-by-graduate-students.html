New Challenges

By NICOLE PAIGE WASILUS on June 23, 2011 7:54 AM | 1 Comment | No TrackBacks

Being surrounded by graduate students has allowed me to "play up a level". Although I am still an undergraduate, this program has thrown me into the graduate level world. It feels great to be taking on more responsibility. At Penn State, I gave the tasks to the preschoolers and I inputted data. Adapting and translating materials was a new responsibility that I took on in the spring semester. And here, it's my job to make sure we are recruiting enough participants, to buy the rewards, set up the lab room, watch the videos, score the data (and repeat). 

It was also great to learn more about the research I was assisting with. Dr. Miller was very patient and explained the purpose of each task and the big picture goal. Now, I have a lot less supervision then at Penn State. The weekly meetings I had with Dr. Miller during the spring semester were crucial. Making most of the adjustments during the school year meant we were able to submit and have the IRB approved before I came to China. That alone saved me a lot of time and worrying here. I also have become a better researcher. I now am more aware of how to better administer the tasks. Taking pauses and speaking slowly are important when dealing with a young child. I've also picked up on how to deal with the more difficult participant (English- or Mandarin- speaking) who is uninterested, shy or has a lot of energy. I think it's important to have as few distractions in the testing room as possible (which me and Bingle found out the hard way!). Removing the other toys in the room that the child may be tempted to get up and play with was a quick solution to that issue. Also, I've tried to make sure that the parent being in the room is not adding any pressure to the child. I usually seat them so they are not looking at the child directly. I've found that the parent's presence is an added bonus when a child can look at his parent for comfort when they are feeling shy or when the parent can encourage the child to pay attention. I've also noticed that although I am rustling through papers and materials, I should keep my eyes on the task going on. When I do that, the child stays focused and doesn't pay attention to me preparing materials.

As I'm sure most PIRE students can attest to, you'll be taking on more responsibility for your project than you ever have before. The best advice I can give is to prepare, prepare, prepare. It is your responsibility to be thinking of issues you will face before you go. Think about every aspect of your project (e.g lab space, recruitment, graduate mentors). Spare no detail. Bring up all of the questions you have before you travel to your destination. That way, with a little bit of luck and a lot of hard work, everything will go smoothly at your foreign site.
