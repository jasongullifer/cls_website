Teaching and learning fNIRS

By Benjamin Zinszer on June 3, 2011 7:44 AM | 2 Comments | No TrackBacks

Almost exactly a year ago, I had the privilege of visiting Drexel University (Philadelphia, PA) to learn about their functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) system. fNIRS has existed as a neuroimaging tool for over 15 years, but only recently has it come to the forefront as a major opportunity for research in cognitive neuroscience, and as specifically a tool for psycholinguistic studies. fNIRS offers the distinct advantages of relative silence and motion insensitivity, permitting speech comprehension and production while undergoing a scan. Like fMRI, it also offers spatial localization of hemodynamic activity, but measuring at considerably higher speeds. A year later, here at Beijing Normal University (literally half a world away), I have had my second chance to work in fNIRS research.

BNU is one of the few academic institutions in China (indeed, one of the few in the world) to house an fNIRS system. They have just begun to use the device in research, as currently only a few faculty and graduate students are familiar with it. I have had the opportunity to be both student and teacher, joining an fNIRS course (admittedly all in Chinese) to learn the experimental and statistical methods used at BNU (ah, the math is quite universal!) and sharing a bit of my own knowledge from my work at Drexel. On Wednesday week I presented in a lab seminar to graduate students who were not familiar with near-infrared spectroscopy. We reviewed the basic physics, experimental technique, a time-domain statistical analysis new to the BNU group, and discussed my ideas for research in bilingual lexical development. (Powerpoint available in repository, password required)


In addition to classroom experience, I'm happy to have joined a graduate student colleague in working with the fNIRS system to implement and test a categorical perception design. Here's a picture of me "trying on" the fNIRS cap. It turns out shaving one's head makes one an ideal candidate for participating in fNIRS studies! The machine is a little imposing at first, but the design is quite intuitive and (believe it or not) quite a lot easier and cleaner (no electro-conductive goo) than ERP setup.
