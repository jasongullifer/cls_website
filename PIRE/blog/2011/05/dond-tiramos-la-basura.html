Donde Tiramos la Basura

By RACHEL ELISABETH NEGRON on May 17, 2011 12:48 PM | No Comments | No TrackBacks

So Kylee and I arrived Saturday morning by a slightly delayed but overall uneventful flight (no screaming babies for once).  The city was different from what we expected, but we are falling in love with it already and, making lots of great friends.  Our favorite friend is this sweet old man that we asked for directions to the beach.  Rosa (our advisor here in Tarragona) told us Catalan would be easy to pick up, so when the little old man began speaking in what I thought was Catalan I thought Rosa is so wrong...I can't understand a word he's saying, all I heard was playa.  Then he told us he was Slovakian; playa must be a cognate.  

Lesson 1: A.  Catalan is very similar to Castilian Spanish and shares a lot of cognates; therefore you can pick up the basics easily.  It's the grammar that is pretty different.

                B.  Never ask /refer to speaking Spanish when referring to Castilian Spanish in Cataluña. Specify with the word Castilian ("actual Spanish") or Catalan (the language spoken in this area) because they are both considered "Spanish".

Rosa has truly been a life saver so far.  When we arrived to our apartment, hotel Lauria, we entered to no blow dryer --as promised (WHAT?!) no internet, a dirty apartment with not bowls to eat our cereal or knife to cut our chicken, no hot water and one tiny double bed (not that Kylee and I mind snuggling together it's just that I woke up with her knee in my back this morning).   They offered to give us a different room but we refused to take it because we are on the top floor of the hotel with two huge balconies that overlook the Mediterranean Sea (future Tarragona scholars request room 45A!).  It's so beautiful at night especially when the oil ships sit out there all lit up (sounds like that would look ugly but it looks really cool!)  Anyway Rosa -who for such a sweet, quiet lady--doesn't take unacceptable behavior from anyone.  She marched over to our hotel this morning and lectured the manager until he came up and felt the cold water and so on.  We now have a mechanic coming to fix the internet, two single beds sitting outside our apartment, hot water and Rosa brought us a knife, bowls and a blow dryer -she really is the best--.

Lesson 2:  to get what you want in Spain you have to be stern and aggressive (not in the physical way!), otherwise -especially as a foreigner--people might try to take advantage of you and get away with it. 

 Today was our first day at the university and everyone is really nice.  Rosa gave us a tour and introduced us to everyone; she even bought us café con leche and a bocadillo.  A technician got our internet set up and we even found a few candidates to participate in our study.   It blows their mind that we will be paying them to participate!  Friday we are excited to be able to present our studies to the psychology department at the Universitat Rovira i Virgili in Tarragona and run our first participants.  Getting the experiments up and running has been a challenge but Mark (our American technician at Penn State) and Marc (our Catalan technician at Universitat Rovira i Virgili) have worked really hard to help us get it all organized on time.

Lesson 3:  Not everything will go smoothly but there are plenty of people there to help you out when they don't, so don't worry!

Oh yea I forgot...our friend Rodger took us out for tapas last night and we tried duck...yea (it was so good!).

Lesson 4:  Don't forget to have fun!
