Participants and Progress

By NATHANIEL JAMES HOLLISTER on May 19, 2011 9:30 AM | 1 Comment | No TrackBacks

After the past few days of craziness, Lauren and I have officially run 24 participants, 17 of which were done this week. I am actually quite proud of that number and I hope that we continue to work so efficiently during the remainder of our stay in Granada. We have a lot more participants scheduled for the upcoming weeks in order to fill our quota of 52 by the time we leave. However, knowing whether or not participants will actually show up is an entirely different story. In fact, the one participant that we had scheduled for today did not show up and actually that is pretty common here. Luckily though, most of the participants that we have had over the last week or so have been very good about coming at their assigned times. And actually, for the most part, we haven't had people coming too late either. The thing that has helped with our participants showing up is the personalized reminder emails that we send to each of them the day before they have to come in. I think if we were just leaving it to their memory, then our number would be a lot lower than it currently is. So, if you seem to be having trouble with actually getting your participants there on experiment day, I would suggest just briefly reminding them.

Read on...

Ultimately, though, the thing that has been making our participants run so smoothly is the support that we have from our host institution and the system that Lauren and I have perfected when running a participant. First, I have found that absolutely everyone here has been very accommodating to our needs and requests. For example, we decided early on that we would be able to participants that came as a group and really just more participants in general, if we had one room for the eye-tracking portion of our study and another one to conduct our behavioral tasks. So, when we asked for two different rooms at the same time, I initially thought that this request would be a problem, but in reality, it turned out to be a very easy feat (yet the system of requesting a room here is a little complicated). Second, Lauren and I have been able to develop a pretty uniform routine that runs like clockwork. Usually, but not always, Lauren will start to run the participant in the eye-tracking room, doing that portion of the study, and then she brings the participant to me in a different room in order to do all of the behavioral tasks that we required be completed with our study. I think that we have each gotten pretty good at our respective instructive tasks, but we still check in with one another about different issues that present themselves and we both know how to do each other's portion as well. I think that this system that we have created is really helping us get through multiple participants in a very short time period.

Unfortunately, this coming week is pretty barren in terms of participants. This is mainly because during that time the new Eyelink 1000 is coming in to replace the current Eyelink 2. For those unfamiliar with the differences in the two systems, I will explain. And if not, you may want to skip the next few lines until you read "End Description". Primarily, the main difference between the two is the placement of the cameras. The Eyelink 2, which is the one that we have been using for the past three weeks, is a helmet-based eye-tracker. Basically the system looks a like a bicycle helmet that has very little protection with two cameras that can be adjusted in the front. On the other hand, the Eyelink 1000 is a stationary system. This system is the one that we use in the CLS labs back home and is placed between the computer screen and the participant, who has his or her head in the headrest. End Description. I have actually become quite accustomed to the Eyelink 2, and will actually be sad to see it go. While both Lauren and I are familiar with the Eyelink 1000, I think that we have gotten pretty good at using the Eyelink 2 over the past few weeks. Nevertheless, I am sure that we will have everything under control and ready to go for our upcoming participants.
