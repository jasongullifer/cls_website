'Hallo' from the Netherlands!

By JAMES RAY GRAHAM on May 5, 2011 11:55 AM | 1 Comment | No TrackBacks
I took my final final exam yesterday, briefly stopped by my house for a last goodbye and a last once-over for anything missing and then went straight to the State College airport. I was amazed by the speed of a plane trip to Philadelphia, this is a route I've only ever driven before.

I always have some anxiety about plane flights, but it's something that's only begun to happen to me in the past three years or so. If I were to psychoanalyze myself, I'd say it's probably me projecting and anxiety about my travel and leaving home onto something more concrete. But as always happens, I arrived safely yet exhausted - at Schiphol Int'l Airport in Amsterdam.

Little sleep in the past few days (including none on the plane) had begun to give my surroundings an edge of surreality. This was compounded by the dawning realization that I was back in Europe, where I sometimes feel I left my heart during my last trip there.

Read on...
Carrying three heavy bags, I purchased a train ticket to Nijmegen and made it there in around an hour and forty minutes. I put my stuff in my room, which is huge, complete with a kitchen, washer/dryer, refrigerator, desk, television, and best of all - a deck. I got some food at the student cafeteria. Cooked vegetables, fried cheese balls, and french fries - a meal not too dissimilar from typical American fare.

Seeing as the Netherlands is a Western country, the similarities are broad and overarching and the difference, for the most part, small. However, it is the differences I always think about and always appreciate. The first thing that strikes me about the Netherlands, as my first time here did two summers ago, is the bicycles. I'm a bicycle-lover and it is something akin to paradise for me to be in a city where they outnumber cars andthe lanes are omnipresent and as big as the roads. When I am on foot, cars going 60 km/hour will slow down and yield to me on a crosswalk. These are examples of what I love the most about this country - progressive politics and an ecologically-friendly ethos, which help to cultivate this pedestrian culture. Nijmegen is very verdant, there are parks, plants, and trees everywhere. The cozy-looking, reasonably-sized houses are all well-kept with greenery covering them and their yards, and always a plant or five in the windowsill (with the curtains generally open - a very Dutch trait). The sun has been out and people are smiling. The streets are clean and virtually free of litter, you'd have to scour to find a cigarette butt or a McDonalds wrapper unlike a street in the United States. Whenever I am in Europe (especially Northern) the shortcomings of the United States manifest themselves strongly and affect me in a way that falls somewhere between jealousy and sadness.

After sleeping for 14 hours (!), getting some groceries and strolling around the area, I am here now writing this! But that's all for now. Talk to you soon, interwebs.
