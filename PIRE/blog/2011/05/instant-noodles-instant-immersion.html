Instant Noodles & Instant Immersion

By Benjamin Zinszer on May 17, 2011 12:27 PM | 6 Comments | No TrackBacks
Between the time zone change, the flights, the layovers, and the short-phase sleep cycles I have recently experienced, it's hard to appreciate that it was only 30 hours ago that Patrick and I left State College on the first leg of our journey to Beijing. I have to say that while I had great ambitions to study my Introduction to Chinese: Level 1 textbook for the duration of the trans-Arctic flight, language immersion was not at the forefront of my thoughts. My multiple trips to Wendy's and the snack shops at Dulles airport probably better exemplifies what I had on my mind yesterday (not to mention the anticipation of knowing that President Spanier and his entourage were also on our flight). But a 14 hour flight is plenty of time for a lot to happen, and this trip has already taken some unexpected (but welcome) turns...
It goes without saying that when I settled down next to my row-mate for the long flight, I was more interested in the menu (and where is that drink cart already?!) than in meeting new people. My immediate concerns were soon satisfied when, an hour into the flight, we were served the first of our three meals (pictured above). Perhaps instant noodles perpetuate the stereotype of questionable airline food, but no graduate student is above a hearty bowl of ramen, especially not me.

The boiling water steamed up with aromas of diminishingly-dehydrated fish and myriad spices, and I started to dig in, almost immediately fumbling my chopsticks, elbowing my neighbor, and causing a general ruckus in row 37. Completely flustered, I turned to the startled Chinese man sitting next to me and attempted to apologize, explaining in Chinese that I was sorry but my arms were too big for this chair. He replied, "I'm sorry, I don't speak English." It would seem that my rendering of the Chinese language was so foreign to him that he assumed it was English, and I, utterly flummoxed, retorted "and I don't speak Chinese."

Clearly off to a great start, I was beginning to dread the prospect of 13 more hours (or 10 more weeks) in this condition. I searched my brain desperately for any Chinese words I was confident I could produce with some degree of accuracy. I honed in on a single candidate, a word which I learned from a two-year-old, and having played his word-game many times, I was certain I had the requisite experience to deliver my monologue successfully. I turned to my neighbor once again, raised my plastic cup of water and pronounced: Gambei! (Cheers!) He responded with a raised cup and a smile. We toasted to... tacit understandings, I suppose, and proceeded with the meal.

What are an English monolingual and a Chinese monolingual to do for 13 hours? We introduced ourselves in a mixture of broken Chinese, broken English, and doodles on the back of my itinerary. (I explained cognitive psychology with a rather fine sketch of the human brain, I might add.) As my friend Xianzhong could now tell you, I "study people who speak Chinese and English." And he works for a company that does something with cameras. A business card and invitation to visit him in Hunan in hand, I had to get a picture of us together before we parted ways at Beijing Capitol Airport. So there it is. My first Chinese immersion experience took place somewhere over northern Canada. If the next two months proceed anything like the first day, I am quite optimistic about future opportunities for language learning.
